# Homepage Copy Review — Conversion Optimization Lens

**Objective**: Review every section of the homepage for emotional resonance, clarity, and conversion effectiveness. The reader should feel: "This is exactly what I need" or "This is what I've been searching for."

**Target audience**: Founders/entrepreneurs who have an offer idea and want to launch a funnel but are overwhelmed by the execution.

---

## Section-by-Section Review

### 1. HERO — Mini-title

**Current**: "For Founders Who Are Ready to Launch"

**Issue (your #1)**: This creates a qualification gate. If someone doesn't feel "ready," they self-select out. The irony is your ideal customer is NOT ready — they're stuck, overwhelmed, and need help getting ready. This line accidentally excludes them.

**Suggestion**: Flip it. Speak to where they ARE, not where they need to be.
- "For Founders With a Funnel to Build"
- "For Founders Who Are Done Waiting"
- "For Founders Who Refuse to Stay Stuck"

The mini-title should make them feel seen, not tested.

---

### 2. HERO — Headline

**Current**: "Your next launch changes everything. It starts with one conversation."

**Issue (your #2)**: "Your next launch" is abstract and future-tense. It doesn't create urgency or personal stakes. The reader hasn't launched yet — "next" implies they've done this before.

**Suggestion**: Make it present-tense and visceral.
- "This is the launch that changes everything." + "It starts with one conversation."
- "Your funnel is closer than you think." + "It starts with one conversation."
- "Everything you need to launch is one conversation away."

The second line ("It starts with one conversation") is strong — it's the hook. The first line needs to match its directness.

---

### 3. HERO — Subheadline

**Current**: "You have the offer. You have the audience. The only thing between you and your first real launch is the 80-hour funnel stack nobody warned you about. That ends here."

**Issue (your #3)**: The assumption that they "have the offer" and "have the audience" may not be true for everyone. More importantly, it doesn't capture the emotional gut-punch of realizing how much work a funnel actually is. The feeling you described — having the momentum and then getting punched in the gut — isn't landing.

**Suggestion**: Lead with the emotional experience, not the logical setup.
- "You had the idea. You had the momentum. Then you sat down to build the funnel and realized it was 80 hours of work nobody warned you about. That wall between you and your first real launch? It just came down."

This version:
- Starts with past tense ("had") — they've already felt this
- Names the emotional moment ("sat down to build")
- Introduces the gut-punch ("80 hours nobody warned you about")
- Resolves it with agency ("It just came down")

---

### 4. STUDIO BENTO — Mini-title

**Current**: "The Product"

**Issue (your #4)**: Generic. Doesn't create curiosity or emotional pull. Every SaaS page has a "product" section.

**Suggestions**:
- "What You Get" — simple, direct, benefit-oriented
- "Your Launch Stack" — names the outcome
- "Inside Funnel Studio™" — creates a peek-behind-the-curtain feeling
- "The System" — implies structure and reliability

---

### 5. EFFICIENCY TABLE — Headline

**Current**: "Three paths. Only one gets you launched."

**Issue (your #5)**: Factually, all three paths CAN get you launched. The claim feels dishonest, and smart readers will notice. It undermines trust at the exact moment you're trying to build it.

**Suggestion**: Reframe around speed/cost/sanity rather than binary success/failure.
- "Three paths to launch. Only one doesn't break you."
- "Three ways to build a funnel. Only one respects your time."
- "Three paths. One takes 80 hours. One costs $10,000. One takes a conversation."

The last option is the strongest — it lets the table prove the point without making a claim the reader might push back on.

---

### 6. BENEFITS — Headline

**Current**: "What your week looks like after the Brain Dump™."

**Issue (your #6)**: Doesn't move the reader emotionally. It's descriptive but not aspirational. "What your week looks like" is mundane — it sounds like a calendar update, not a life change.

**Suggestions**:
- "What changes after one conversation." — simple, powerful, ties back to the hero
- "The week everything finally clicks." — emotional, aspirational
- "This is what launching actually feels like." — reframes the experience

---

### 7. PROBLEM — "The Blank Page" card

**Current title**: "The Blank Page"
**Current body**: "You open the AI tool, hit generate, and the output reads like it was written for nobody. Thin. Generic. Not even close to what you needed."

**Issue (your #7)**: The title says "Blank Page" but the body describes bad AI output, not a blank page. The metaphor doesn't match the content. A blank page is about staring at nothing. Bad AI output is about getting something useless.

**Suggestions**:
- Rename to "The AI Letdown" or "The Generic Output" — matches the body copy
- OR keep "The Blank Page" and rewrite the body: "You sit down to write your squeeze page. The cursor blinks. You know what you want to say but the words won't come. An hour later, the page is still empty."

Either fix works — just make the title and body tell the same story.

---

### 8. FOUNDER — Closing line

**Current**: "All 100 founding spots are available until they are gone."

**Issue (your #8)**: Redundant. "Available until they are gone" is a tautology — of course they're available until they're not. It doesn't add urgency, it adds awkwardness.

**Suggestions**:
- "100 founding spots. Once they are gone, the price goes to $149/mo."
- "The founding rate is open to the first 100. After that, it is gone."
- Simply remove the line — the pricing section already handles scarcity.

---

### 9. CTA BUTTONS — Scarcity subtext

**Issue (your #9)**: The two mid-page CTA buttons (after the comparison table and before the footer) have no scarcity reinforcement. The hero CTA has "$149/mo after founding spots fill" and the pricing card has the "Limited to 100 Spots" badge, but the mid-page CTAs are naked.

**Suggestion**: Add a small subtext line under each mid-page CTA button:
- "Limited to the first 100 founding members" or
- "Founding rate — 100 spots available"

This creates consistent scarcity pressure throughout the scroll without being aggressive.

---

## Additional Observations

### 10. SECTION ORDER — Problem comes after Benefits

The Problem section ("Sound Familiar?") comes AFTER the Benefits section. In classic conversion copywriting, you agitate the problem BEFORE presenting the solution/benefits. The current order is:

1. Hero (promise)
2. Product showcase (solution)
3. Comparison table (proof)
4. Benefits (what changes)
5. Problem (pain) ← this should come earlier
6. Founder (trust)
7. Objections (doubt removal)
8. Pricing (action)
9. Final CTA

**Consideration**: Moving Problem before Benefits (or even before the comparison table) would follow the classic Problem → Agitation → Solution flow. The reader would feel the pain, then see the relief. Currently they see the relief first, then the pain — which is backwards emotionally.

However, this is a structural change and your current flow does work — the product showcase immediately after the hero is compelling. This is more of a "if you want to optimize further" note.

### 11. COMPARISON TABLE — "The Real Cost" mini-title

"The Real Cost" as a mini-title is strong. No change needed.

### 12. OBJECTIONS — Solid

The objections section is well-written. The questions are real, the answers are direct and confident. No changes recommended.

### 13. FINAL CTA — "Now finish the funnel."

"You have the offer. You have the audience. Now finish the funnel." — This is strong. It mirrors the hero language and creates a bookend. The only note is the same one from the hero: "You have the offer. You have the audience." may not be true for everyone. But at this point in the page, the reader has been through the full story, so it works better here than in the hero.
